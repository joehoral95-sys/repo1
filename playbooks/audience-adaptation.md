# Audience adaptation

The same data makes a different deck for different rooms. Fill `audience` and
`intent` in the deck meta first; they select everything below.

## SOA audience profiles

### Board of Directors
- **Care about:** strategy, risk, money, member trust, "are we on plan."
- **Framing:** decision-first (BLUF), options with a recommendation,
  objections pre-answered. They are numbers-fluent — don't over-explain, but
  do state materiality ("-$1.2M vs plan").
- **Length/density:** short deck, high stakes per slide. `comparison` for the
  decision, `big_number` for status, one `chart` per claim that needs proof.
- **Avoid:** operational detail, unexplained jargon from one department,
  suspense.

### All-staff / town hall
- **Care about:** what it means for them, momentum, being trusted with real
  news, recognition.
- **Framing:** story arc over pyramid — where we were, what happened, where
  we're going. Celebrate concretely (name teams). Deliver bad news plainly
  with the response plan next to it.
- **Length/density:** more visual variety (`icon_row`, `quote`, `timeline`,
  hero `big_number`), `animate: build` welcome. Fewer decimals: "about
  two-thirds," not 66.4%.
- **Avoid:** board-speak ("EBITDA-adjusted"), walls of bullets, spin —
  staff detect it instantly.

### Members / external professional audience
- **Care about:** the profession's health, what SOA does for them, credibility.
- **Framing:** evidence-forward, sources cited (`source:` on charts),
  measured tone. The SOA brand carries formality — lean classy.
- **Avoid:** internal metrics that don't touch members, triumphalism.

### Committees / working groups
- **Care about:** the specific matter, completeness, being consulted.
- **Framing:** more `table` and detail is appropriate here than anywhere
  else; appendices welcome. Still assertion titles.

## The three calibration questions

1. **What do they already know?** Everything they know is one recap slide,
   not a section.
2. **What do they need to feel vs. decide?** Town halls need feeling
   (momentum, honesty); boards need decision scaffolding (options, criteria,
   ask). Mixed rooms: decide for the highest-stakes participant.
3. **What's the one thing they'll repeat afterward?** Design the deck so it's
   the thing you chose, not an accident. Make that the title-test spine.

## Intent patterns

- **Approve X** → decision framing: status → case → options (`comparison`) →
  ask (`quote`) → timeline of what approval unlocks.
- **Inform / reassure** → status framing: headline KPIs up front, trends,
  risks with mitigations, no fake ask at the end.
- **Rally / motivate** → story framing: challenge → what we did → proof
  (`big_number`) → what's next, warmer tone throughout.
- If Joe's request mixes intents, ask which one wins; a deck that half-asks
  and half-rallies does neither.
